### What motivates me to write this post, and why you should read this post

Taking the current times, almost everyone is talking about AI, and most of them are more experienced and much more insightful than me. One of the reasons to actually talk on this topic is the fact that everyone has something to share.

Everyone has used AI to build something which they were never able to build before. Non-programmers were able to build new applications overnight and got termed as <i>vibe-coders</i>. Expert programmers were able to transform their already so-powerful applications into extremely powerful ones. And then comes the middle — programmers who are better than non-programmers but less experienced than the experts. I can very confidently say that I lie in exactly this bracket, and if you have developed a few projects before the beginning of the AI-race, most likely you fall here too.

So the main reason I'm writing this post — and one of the reasons you might want to read it — is to talk about how AI has affected our journey to becoming expert programmers. Both for the good and for the bad. I can present my own journey as a real example of how AI will affect us, and how I was able to use it to build something which I can confidently say ninety percent of the population cannot build by using mere AI alone. I know that's a hard claim. But I can actually show where AI helped and where I had to step in myself. So wear your seatbelts, and with a clear mind, try to relate to what I have to share.

### Where did us Humans Lose?

Looking at my experiences with AI, I can very confidently assume AI is not getting too powerful at solving problems or building billion-dollar products. It is getting too powerful at saving time.

> I might sound a bit inexperienced at what I write, but you might be able to relate it with what you do. This entire section is merely based on what I have done.

Most of the stuff we do today, or while working, is building which is termed as development. When we talk about development, most of it is just a bunch of repeated patterns and pre-existing code.

Talking about frontend web-development — the same Buttons, the same Tailwind classes, the same TypeScript functions. Everything, all of it, has already been written by another programmer and is lying in some open-source GitHub repository or a self-hosted Git instance somewhere on the internet. AI barely looked at it once or twice, and hell yeah, we do not have to write it for the hundredth time. Humans lost in typing speed. They were writing the same thing again and again, and AI came and wrote that thing for them.

Coming to backend web-development, the same API endpoints, the same hash function, the same database queries. Everything has been done before in some orphaned Git repository hosted online. AI just looked at it twice or thrice, used its own knowledge base and tuned its parameters. Now when you wanted that basic API endpoint in your project, AI did it for you.

Coming to the DevOps pipeline, when you wanted the same GitHub CI/CD workflow to test your code against some bash script you wrote — to run on push or on a pull request — AI came to the rescue. Millions of projects have already written the same script, in a different fashion, but achieved whatever you wanted. You might have wanted a green checkmark or a red cross, and a million other people already earned it. You barely had the same repetitive test and AI wrote the workflow for you.

Coming to Native development, you wanted to launch an application on Android, iOS, macOS, Windows or some Linux variant, and you got it. Thousands of applications have already launched on the OS you are targeting. Thousands of applications have already achieved the same login flow, the same responses on button presses, and the same API calls on request.

Coming to product development, thousands of text editors, browsers, and what not, are already open-sourced. All LLMs are already fine-tuned to those open-sourced or public codebases. What you are writing is basically what they have already written — tried and tested.

AI doesn't have the feelings of completion, but us Humans, we have. When you write whatever has already been done by millions of developers already, what you are basically getting is the feeling that you have achieved something. But what you have built is basically a fixed foundation. A wooden building.

What you forgot to do is fill it with concrete on the supporting sticks. Build the roof. Build a foundation so that when you scale, when you expand, the building should not fall off. A room can't be built unless you finish the building.

Us, the Humans, we lost in completing the building. We built the same wooden structure millions of projects already have inside. The mere requirements to start building something new. For the LLM, for the AI, it is complete — because it was self-trained. We receive a sense of completion from merely a black box which notifies us that <b><i>"Whatever you have instructed me to do is complete"</i></b>. We never go beyond that.

We just saved our time building the wooden structure, so that we can invest more of it putting the concrete in. Making floors. Making roofs. And eventually, making the rooms.

### Where did the AI Lose?

This would come off as something you might have heard over a thousand times — maybe in some YouTube video, maybe in some X post, or who knows where. But this idea, the core notion, would have definitely reached your mind.

People generally say that AI will never reach Human intelligence, but when I think about it, that line between machine intelligence and Human intelligence is getting blurry the more we step into the future (has the future I am referring to been reached?). AI has been quite good at reaching answers and making decisions on its own, and it makes us really proud. But there is a catch. AI has been trained on all the stuff Human intelligence has built till now. That basically means AI has reached a point where it can replicate all the human intelligence it has ever seen.

Now when I refer to the previous section — AI, when trained on documentation carefully crafted by Humans, applies its own form of understanding natural language and forms its own way of solving something when instructed. It passes the "prompt" into the blackbox and produces an output. When you ask AI to optimize your code, it will look for everything it has ever learnt related to optimization and try to match it against the code snippet you provided, based on the probabilistic model it has built itself. Keep in mind the term probabilistic. It means it applies the rules of probability to check what fits best. Out of the millions of snippets it has seen, if half a million use `useMemo` instead of something else — `useCallback`, `useEffect`, anything — and have documented (not achieved) a significant amount of performance improvements, it will add to its knowledge base that `useMemo` is better in almost all cases. The knowledge base itself is the issue.

Here, exactly here, is where AI loses.

AI never lost in performing the task. AI lost because it was trying to replicate a Human Intelligence step it was never meant to do or understand — just because of your bad instruction: "Optimize this code snippet." AI lost in doing something it was vaguely instructed to.

#### A Trip to History

I will take a step back and take you into history. It was 1997. Deep Blue vs Garry Kasparov. It was talked about as "The Brain's Last Stand" — a tagline that perfectly captured the public's anxiety that human intellectual dominance was about to end.

Most of you would think that the AI won, but it was the opposite. Kasparov won against Deep Blue and proved that Human intelligence would still prevail. Coming to today, Human intelligence is nowhere near a Chess Computer's intelligence, even after putting the computer in extremely hard constraints.

The only reason Kasparov won in 1997 was not because the computer was weak. In fact, the computer was extremely strong — using state-of-the-art methods like iterative deepening to calculate the best move in the shortest amount of nodes visited, saving time on the clock. It lost because the mathematics was wrong. The evaluation function being used was not correctly calculating the positional scores of a certain chess position. It was not considering a winning position, and was choosing a worse position instead, because the worse position was ranked higher in its blackbox. The evaluation function — the "Brain of the Chess Engine" — was not something the engine wrote. It was something humans wrote.

#### The Present

Now, why did I take this example? When we refer to the evaluation function in the current context, it is the prompt you gave to the AI: "Optimize this code snippet." The AI, now in the process of optimizing, is not considering a better optimization — although it is applying state-of-the-art methods — just because the evaluation function you gave, the prompt you gave, is not correctly calculating what needs to be done and what is better.

It has the knowledge of the entire world.

It lost because it has the knowledge of the entire world.

A better evaluation function would have been: "Optimize this code snippet so that it doesn't make redundant API calls." Or: "Optimize this code snippet so that the component does not re-render on every variable change."

This is where us Humans win. The Human Brain has a very interesting property — it goes into tunnel vision when it comes to survival. The Brain will almost instantly know the weak point because it can filter out much quicker than the AI. In sleep, the Human Brain rewires itself using almost negligible energy, so that the next day you wake up, you can decide what to do and what not to do in lesser time compared to the previous day. The AI takes months to do this, again failing to achieve sub-par results. This does not make the AI weaker on its own. It just makes it weaker if the Human operating it is not knowledgeable enough to make a better evaluation function.

Coming to the first section I wrote — a non-programmer has no idea how to make a better evaluation function. An expert programmer has all the knowledge in the world to build the best evaluation functions. And us programmers who know something, we can build a good enough one. Where we fail to build an evaluation function, the AI fails instantly — and worse than us in most cases.

#### How I was able to build something which ninety percent of the people would be unable to build with AI alone

> Although I can't share much details, I can share an outline of what I have been building.

What I and two of my friends have been working on is a programming language and its compiler. Now, you might have seen or known dozens of languages in your lifetime, but building one is a trip to Mars. The cherry on top: building something like this in the AI-era, where almost every language is open-source, might feel very easy.

You tell the AI: "Help me make a compiler from scratch."

The AI, already trained on multiple languages, will build you exactly these: A `Lexer`, A `Parser`, A `Syntax Validator`, A `Semantic Validator`, An `IR Generator`, A `Code Optimizer` and a `Code Generator`. And when it is done, a new programming language is done. Millions of people have done this.

But if it were this easy, there would be hundreds of thousands of mainstream languages produced every single month. If a compiler were just these six files, it would be the easiest job in the market. But here we are — there can only be so many languages that exist.

The reason is not that this is a hard job. The reason is the Evaluation Function I have been talking about in this section. Any AI can tell you the six phases of a compiler and how to code it. There even exist Parser Generators and Lexical Analyzer Generators that the AI might suggest to save time. You might even choose them. The only reason you will lose is the fact that you will go with what the AI says <b>blindly</b>. You will choose the Parser Generator and Lexical Analyzer Generator blindly.

The only reason you will win — and I won — is the fact that I was able to build a better evaluation function.

This better evaluation function only comes from the knowledge you already have. The knowledge I already had was about a Lexer, a Parser, all the types of Parsers: `LL(1)`, `LR(0)`, `CLR(1)`, `SLR(1)`, `LALR(1)` and Pratt Parsing (an improved Operator Precedence Parser), an actual Syntax Validator, an actual Semantic Validator, an IR Generator, all the Code Optimization techniques (Dead Code Elimination, Constant Folding, Loop Unrolling, Loop Tiling and tens of others) and finally LLVM.

This way, I did not have to blindly follow any AI and was able to lay down a plan myself. The only place where I took something from the AI was the implementation of the Parser I already knew about, the implementation of the Lexical Analyzer I already knew about, making documentation of the stuff I knew about, and taking notes of everything being done — so that I don't get lost later in development, and so that there could be a single source of truth for everything being built.

When it comes to the phases of compilation, it takes hours of research to get to know what is actually being done by real compiler engineers and maintainers. Reading their engineering blogs, and even talking to the AI about how things are actually done before completely submitting myself and my project to it — that is the main task. And that is what ninety percent of the people will be unable to do with AI alone.

AI would never tell you in the first pass whether this language, or whatever it has built, is real and production-grade ready. The codebase needs hundreds of iterations on the same line, the same block of code, to actually get to something useful and error-free. My teammates, although working continuously, sometimes fumble into this exact problem — but quickly recover.

What is the <b>problem</b>? Blindly accepting the wooden structure the AI gave and then trying to live in it. It is doomed to collapse. If you are unable to form the concrete, your building is doomed to get demolished when you try to live inside it. To lay the foundation and put the concrete in, you need the knowledge — the bare minimum knowledge you need to have. It is knowing how everyone else is doing it. The AI already knows. It is you who has to make the evaluation function.

The AI already knows how to build a `Resolver`, how to build a `TypeChecker`. It even knows that these are building blocks of a compiler, theoretically abstracted to the mere six phases. But it is you who has to poke it. This is exactly where I won, and where everyone — even my teammates — need to improve. The actual will to know, and thus be able to build a better evaluation function.

You will be able to learn something only if you know how it is built, because the AI already knows. The moment you learn the actual theory and lay the foundation by learning it, eventually you will be able to build something like what we are building — or even better. Because the Human Brain will resort to tunnel vision, getting exactly what it wants merely to survive and solve the problem.

The AI is a CPU attached to a RAM and having an SSD. The Human Brain is a SSD which is also a RAM, and the RAM itself is the CPU. This is the architectural difference. This is something which is still keeping Humans in control.

### Final Thoughts

This article is quite a long one, but I wrote almost everything I had in my mind. Since AI is a hot topic right now, I wanted to add my own thoughts to it. I have been a daily user of AI — especially Claude for coding. I can never imagine a day of development without it. But it always comes with consequences.

Although we are using AI in our everyday tasks (do not lie to yourself — you might also be using it, even subconsciously), we do not want it to take over us. To keep yourself in control, you do not need to control your usage of AI. Your usage automatically dials itself down subconsciously. What I mean by this is — if you are continuously building projects, your AI usage will definitely grow linearly with it. If you are delivering products continuously, your usage will spike with it. Eventually, your AI usage will saturate to the amount of work you do. It will stagnate at some point. You might have even already reached that saturation.

But the actual thing you need to take away from this post is this — never submit yourself to AI. Never end up with a wooden structure and call it a day. It is not your finished product. It is merely a prototype. Your AI will know how to finalize it with concrete. It is you who is the civil engineer guiding it. You will be the one who knows which part of the AI to poke.

Be in control. Your Brain is the single most advantage keeping you in control. Store the most amount of useful information you can in your SSD, so that you can use the tunnel vision your Brain provides — in the scenarios where you need it the most.

With this, peace out!
